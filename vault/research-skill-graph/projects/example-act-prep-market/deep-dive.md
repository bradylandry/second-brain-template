# Deep Dive — ACT Prep Market Research

## Lens 1: Technical

### Participation & Scores
- Louisiana: ~50,000 juniors/year take ACT (98% participation, state-mandated since 2013)
- Louisiana average composite: ~18.2 (ranked 47th nationally)
- National average: ~19.4 (lowest since 1991)
- Majority of Louisiana students score below 20 — meaning below TOPS Opportunity threshold
- Sweet spot for prep (17–22 scorers): estimated 30–40% of cohort = 14,000–20,000 students/yr

### Score Improvement From Prep
- Retesting alone (no prep): +1–3 composite points average
- Structured prep (12–16 hours): +3–5 points
- Extended prep (40+ hours): +4–6 points
- Gains >7 points are rare at the population level
- **Most important:** the 17–22 cohort is where gains are most accessible — lower starting points have more room to improve on learnable content

### Best Evidence-Based Study Methods (ranked by efficacy)
1. **Retrieval practice** — active recall beats re-reading by 2-3x for retention
2. **Spaced repetition (FSRS)** — outperforms massed practice by 200–400%; FSRS achieves 89.6% recall accuracy
3. **Interleaving** — mixing section types improves score transfer by ~30%; almost no app does this
4. **Timed practice under real conditions** — essential for pacing, which is a major failure mode
5. **Error analysis** — reviewing wrong answers with explanations is more valuable than doing more questions

### Highest ROI Sections
- **English:** grammar rules are learnable and finite (commas, apostrophes, sentence structure)
- **Math:** algebra + geometry rules are learnable; calculator vs. no-calculator strategy matters
- **Reading + Science:** slower to improve; depend on speed and pattern recognition; lower ROI for short prep windows

### Tension with Contrarian Lens
The technical evidence says FSRS is the right mechanism. The contrarian lens challenges whether AI tutoring (vs. a pure question bank) actually moves scores. Current evidence supports FSRS > AI tutoring for outcome. AI tutor is a retention mechanism, not a score mechanism.

| Princeton Review | $299–$949 | Web + live |
| Private tutoring | $60–$200/hr | 1:1 |

**Gap:** No paid product priced $20–$99. That zone is unoccupied and where this app should live.

### TOPS Financial Stakes
- TOPS Opportunity (ACT 20+): ~$44,000 over 4 years at LSU
- TOPS Performance (ACT 23+): ~$44,000 + stipend
- TOPS Honors (ACT 27+): ~$44,000 + larger stipend
- The 19→20 crossing is a binary $44,000 event. A $49 app offering this path = 900x ROI framing.

### Monetization
- Freemium conversion in ed-tech: 2–4% realistic, 5–8% best case
- Free trial conversion: ~22%
- Louisiana addressable paying users at 3%: 420–600/yr → $20,000–$30,000/yr (Louisiana only)
- **This confirms: national expansion is required for meaningful revenue**
- Recommended model: free diagnostic + sample plan; paid = adaptive FSRS engine + AI tutor + full question bank

### B2B
- School district procurement: real opportunity but slow (6–18 month cycles)
- Faster path: school counselors recommending app directly (B2B2C)
- Title I / ESSA funding pathway exists for research-certified tools (longer-term)

## Lens 3: Historical

### ACT Participation Trends
- National participation declining (1.9M → 1.4M since test-optional movement)
- Louisiana stable at ~98% due to state mandate — structurally protected from national trend
- Test-optional movement peaked ~2024 at 2,025+ schools; elite schools (Harvard, MIT, Yale, Dartmouth) reinstating requirements

### Khan Academy SAT Precedent (2015)
Khan Academy launched official free SAT prep with College Board endorsement. Industry predicted death of paid prep. Didn't happen. Kaplan survived. Magoosh grew. Reason: free tools have high churn, no personalization, no accountability. Same dynamic applies to ACT.

### Ed-Tech Failures — Knewton
- Raised $180M, partnered with Pearson, promised AI-adaptive learning for all
- Failed 2019: overpromised AI, depended on publisher partners who became competitors, collected data aggressively, couldn't prove outcomes
- Lesson: prove outcomes with real score data, stay lean, don't depend on single partner

### TOPS History
- Created 1997–1998; ACT score thresholds unchanged in 20+ years
- Survived multiple budget crises; partial funding cuts in 2016–2017 restored
- Core structure is legislatively protected and politically popular
- Low but real risk: Louisiana budget shortfalls can cause payment delays

## Lens 4: Geopolitical / Structural

### Louisiana Mandatory ACT
- Mandated since 2013, funded via state initiative + ESSA/federal Title I/Title II funds
- BESE policy reinforced in 2017–2018
- Serves state workforce development goals, college access equity, federal accountability
- Not going away — politically popular, federally supported

### ACT Inc. Conflict of Interest
- ACT Inc. simultaneously: test administrator + prep publisher + Kaplan's exclusive live-prep partner (since 2016)
- Structurally incentivized for students to need retakes
- Independent app positioning as student-first is a genuine differentiation angle

### Test-Optional Is Irrelevant for This Market
- Louisiana TOPS requires ACT regardless of what colleges do
- Target schools (LSU, ULL, UNO, Southern) all still use ACT
- Test-optional only affects elite schools the TOPS cohort mostly doesn't target

## Lens 5: Contrarian

### The Hard Challenges

**TAM is smaller than it looks**
Louisiana-only at 3% conversion = modest revenue. Must expand nationally.

**Prep has noisy individual results**
Population-level averages (+3–5 pts) mask real variance. Some students see 0 improvement. Students with genuine skill deficits (not just test-taking gaps) need content remediation, not just strategy.

**AI tutor ≠ score improvement (yet)**
FSRS question bank is the proven mechanism. AI tutor drives engagement. Don't over-invest in AI at the expense of question quality.

**Mobile is right for daily reviews, wrong for test simulation**
Students should practice full-length tests on paper or desktop. Mobile-only prep doesn't simulate real test conditions. App should explicitly direct users to print/desktop for full practice tests.

**Free tools don't make paid impossible — but they raise the bar**
Must offer something free tools can't: TOPS framing, score guarantee, adaptive personalization, better UX.

## Lens 6: First Principles

### What Does a Student Need to Go from 19 to 21?
- ~20–30 focused hours over 6–10 weeks
- Identify 2–3 specific sub-skill gaps (not just "English is weak" but "I miss comma splice and run-on questions")
- Drill those sub-skills via retrieval practice until automaticity
- Learn 4–5 ACT-specific test strategies (pacing, process of elimination, trap answers)
- Take 2–3 full-length timed practice tests

### Minimum Viable Product for Reliable Score Improvement
1. Diagnostic → identify weakest sub-skills
2. FSRS question bank organized by sub-skill
3. Interleaved practice (mixed sections)
4. Error analysis with detailed explanations
5. Progress tracker tied to TOPS thresholds
6. Full-length timed practice test (printable or desktop)

### What Students Hate About Existing Apps
- "No good explanations for why wrong answers are wrong"
- "Can't find what I'm weak at — just shows me a score"
- "Too long — no time for a 3-hour practice test on a weeknight"
- "Free version is useless, paid version isn't much better"
- "Boring — feels like homework"
- "Questions aren't hard enough to prepare for the real test"

### What Best-Rated Apps Do (UWorld, PrepScholar, Magoosh)
- Harder-than-real-test questions (positive transfer effect)
- Detailed explanations with visuals for wrong answers
- Sub-skill performance dashboard (not just a composite score)
- Adaptive study plan that updates as you improve
- Risk-reducing guarantee (Magoosh: 4-point improvement or refund)
