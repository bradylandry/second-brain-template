# Lens: Economic

Follow the money. Who pays, who profits, what incentives drive behavior?

## Core Questions
1. Who pays for this, and how much?
2. Who profits, and what are their margins?
3. What incentive structures drive behavior (students, parents, schools, tutors)?
4. What has been tried commercially and what failed?
5. What is the total addressable market?
6. What pricing models exist and why?

## ACT-Specific Questions
- What is the ACT prep market size globally and in Louisiana specifically?
- What do existing competitors charge? (Kaplan, Princeton Review, Khan Academy, ACT Academy, PrepScholar)
- What do parents actually pay for tutoring vs. apps vs. courses? Price sensitivity?
- What is the TOPS scholarship worth financially? Does that change willingness to pay?
- What does a 1-point ACT score increase mean economically for a Louisiana student?
- How do schools budget for test prep? Are there B2B opportunities (school district contracts)?
- What monetization models have worked in ed-tech? Which have failed?
- What is the LTV of a student user? (prep window = ~12-18 months)

## How to Research Through This Lens
- Follow incentives, not stated motivations
- Look for: pricing pages, revenue figures, funding announcements, market research reports
- Find who is being underserved economically (too expensive, wrong pricing model)
- Check ed-tech investor theses — they've already done some of this work

## Output Format
For each finding:
- **MARKET:** [segment or player]
- **ECONOMICS:** [price, revenue, margin, or spend]
- **INCENTIVE:** [what drives this behavior]
- **SOURCE:** [with tier]

Record all figures in [[../knowledge/data-points]].

## Voice
Follow the money without moralizing. State incentives as facts, not judgments. "Kaplan charges $1,499 for a 6-week course, implying ~$250/hr equivalent" not "Kaplan is too expensive."
